## Prologue: Schizophonic Mimesis

The structural richness of non-Western music has not protected it from exploitation. On the contrary, the "exotic" textures of the BaAka and the "primal" rhythms of the Gitxsan have made them targets for what ethnomusicologist Steven Feld calls **schizophonic mimesis** — the splitting of sounds from their source and their recontextualization into global commodities.

What follows are three case studies in sonic extraction, and one courtroom where a nation's song was refused admission as law.

---

## I. Herbie Hancock and the Hindewhu

In 1973, Herbie Hancock released *Head Hunters*, featuring the iconic track "Watermelon Man." The song's opening — a musician blowing into a beer bottle while singing — replicates the **hindewhu** technique of the BaBenzélé people of the Central African Republic.

Hancock had heard the technique on an ethnomusicological LP recorded by Simha Arom. While his use was an act of Afro-centric creative reclamation — a Black American artist reaching back toward African roots — the original BaBenzélé performers were **neither credited nor compensated.** The sound was treated as raw material. As nature. Not as intellectual property belonging to specific human beings with names and communities.

The hindewhu is not a generic "African sound." It is a **named technique** within a specific cultural tradition, requiring years of practice, governed by rules of performance context, and integral to the spiritual life of the BaBenzélé. Treating it as ambient texture is the sonic equivalent of mining a sacred mountain for gravel.

---

## II. Deep Forest and the Erasure of Afunakwa

In 1992, the French duo Deep Forest released "Sweet Lullaby," a global techno hit built on a sampled vocal line. The album's marketing leaned heavily on "Pygmy" imagery, describing the music as **"the ancestral sound of the rainforest."**

There was one problem: the sample was not Pygmy music.

It was a recording of a woman named **Afunakwa** from the Solomon Islands (Melanesia), singing a lullaby called "Rorogwela." The recording had been made by ethnomusicologist Hugo Zemp for a UNESCO LP. Deep Forest misidentified the culture entirely — erasing Afunakwa's identity, her island, her people — and profited immensely while the Solomon Islands community received nothing.

> Non-Western sounds became **interchangeable signifiers of primitivism.** It did not matter whether the voice was from Central Africa or Melanesia. To the Western ear, it was all "exotic."

---

## III. The Chain of Imitation

In 1994, Madonna sampled Hancock's "Watermelon Man" for her track "Sanctuary." She legally cleared the sample with Hancock and Sony Music, paying royalties to the jazz musician.

The irony is surgical: **the Western copy was treated as property worthy of compensation, while the original indigenous source remained invisible and unpaid.** This created a chain of imitation in which value accumulated exclusively in the hands of Western artists:

1. BaBenzélé hindewhu (uncredited, uncompensated)
2. → Hancock samples it (becomes copyrighted jazz)
3. → Madonna samples Hancock (pays Hancock royalties)

At each link, the legal and economic infrastructure of Western intellectual property law recognized the latest iteration as the "original" and erased everything upstream. The forest's voice was laundered through the studio system until it became someone else's property.

---

## IV. Delgamuukw v. The Queen: When Song Was Refused as Law

The most devastating case of sonic colonialism did not occur in a recording studio. It occurred in a **courtroom.**

In the landmark Canadian case *Delgamuukw v. The Queen* (1987–1997), the Gitxsan and Wet'suwet'en nations sued for ownership of their unceded territories. To prove their title to the land, they presented their **adaawk** — oral histories encoded in song — as evidence.

Chief Mary Johnson (Antgulilibix) prepared to perform her **limx oo'y**, a dirge song that functioned as a legal deed. In Gitxsan jurisprudence, the song *is* the title. It contains the genealogy, the territorial boundaries, the history of stewardship. It is not a supplement to a written document — it **is** the document.

Chief Justice Allan McEachern dismissed the offer:

> *"I have a tin ear... it's not going to do any good to sing to me."*

He refused to hear the song as law, categorizing it as "performance" or "folklore" — inadmissible in a property dispute governed by written documentation.

---

## V. The Settler Ear

Dylan Robinson's concept of **Hungry Listening** (*shxwelítemelh xwélalà:m*) names what happened in that courtroom. It describes the settler-colonial mode of perception: a way of listening that seeks to **extract, categorize, and consume** indigenous sound while refusing to engage with its ontological or legal reality.

Robinson distinguishes between two modes:

**Guest listening** — approaching another culture's sound with openness, accepting that its meaning and function may exceed your categories, that it may operate as law, as medicine, as ecological negotiation, in ways you cannot immediately comprehend.

**Extraction listening** — approaching sound as raw material to be harvested, classified, and integrated into your own frameworks. The Deep Forest producer extraction-listens. The court extraction-listens. The ethnomusicologist who records without reciprocity extraction-listens.

McEachern's refusal was not a personal failing. It was a **structural act of epistemic violence.** By imposing a "settler ear" that can only recognize written documents as truth, the court delegitimized an entire legal order and facilitated the dispossession of land.

---

## VI. Beyond Rhythm: The African Imagination

Kofi Agawu extends the critique to musicology itself. He argues that Western scholarship has **essentialized** African music by focusing obsessively on "rhythm" — the "rhythm complex" that paints African sound as purely corporeal, instinctual, and pre-intellectual.

This framing denies the **imaginative agency** of African composers. It treats them as "informants" for Western theory rather than as **theorists in their own right** — people who have developed sophisticated analytical frameworks for understanding their own music, frameworks that Western musicology has systematically ignored.

> The moment we describe African music as "rhythmically complex but harmonically simple," we have committed an act of epistemic violence. We have imposed our hierarchy of parameters and found the other tradition wanting by our own standards.

---

## Epilogue: The Forest Remembers

The BaAka believe that when misfortune strikes, the forest is asleep, and only the beauty of polyphonic song — the **Yelli** — can wake it. The Gitxsan believe that the song contains the land.

In neither tradition is music entertainment. It is **law. It is ecology. It is the connective tissue between the human and the more-than-human world.**

Every time a Western producer samples a forest voice without credit, every time a court refuses to hear a song as a deed, the connective tissue is severed. The forest sleeps a little deeper. The land loses a little more of its voice.

But the forest remembers the songs. It always has.
