## I. The Premise

The world is described as more dangerous than in decades. Prime ministers are clear: *"Now it's serious."* The message is massive and unanimous — we must prepare for conflict.

But beneath the rhetoric lies a deeper crisis: **the international rules-based order is disintegrating.** When the world's largest military power suggests that "might is right," small states become pawns in a game they lose militarily regardless.

In this picture, increased militarization appears as the only answer. The knee-jerk reaction is to meet force with force.

But looking at classical logic, ethics, and modern research, a very different conclusion emerges: **pacifism is not just a moral ideal for peacetime — it is the most rational strategy for reducing the risk of catastrophe.**

---

## II. The Negative Sum

In game theory, terminology is everything. A **zero-sum game** means one's gain is another's loss. But the atomic age has introduced something far worse: the **negative-sum game**, where destruction is so total that the sum becomes negative for *all* participants. There are no winners — only losers.

The logic behind deterrence ignores a mathematical fact: **the probability of the negative-sum outcome increases with every new weapon deployed.**

Robert Axelrod's research on cooperation demonstrates that mutual trust over time produces better results than mutual threat. The only strategy that consistently avoids total loss is a coordinated movement toward disarmament.

> Knowledge and culture have a unique property: **they do not diminish when shared — they multiply.** When we share our expressions and values, total understanding increases for all parties.

---

## III. The Cold Calculation

Opponents of pacifism invoke the worst case: *what if we're invaded?*

Here fear must be met with logic. Today's authoritarian states rarely seek total annihilation — they seek **control over resources and influence.** This distinction is critical.

Bertrand Russell argued during the Cold War that in a time of weapons of mass destruction, **a temporary occupation is rationally preferable to total war.** Democracy and freedom are robust ideas that survive oppression and can be regained — but they cannot emerge from annihilation.

Gene Sharp — "nonviolence's Machiavelli" — demonstrated that occupying powers depend entirely on the population's cooperation. Through systematic civil disobedience, a country can be made **ungovernable without destroying its cities.** Erica Chenoweth and Maria Stephan confirmed this empirically: across 300+ conflicts from 1900 to 2006, **nonviolent campaigns were twice as effective as violent uprisings** at achieving political change.

Choosing not to retaliate militarily is not surrender. It is a calculated strategy to preserve the nation's most important resource: **its people.**

---

## IV. The Biology of Cooperation

Robert Sapolsky, in *Behave*, describes how violence is biologically rooted: we are capable of great empathy *and* of violence under acute threat. This makes aggression understandable — **but not inevitable.** Our biology gives us equal capacity for cooperation, and the context we create determines which side dominates.

Rutger Bregman reminds us in *Humankind* that there exists a deep psychological barrier against taking life — soldiers in war historically shot over the enemy's heads or refrained from shooting without massive conditioning. **Cooperation, not cynicism, has been our species' greatest strength.**

Martin Nowak at Harvard has shown mathematically that in complex systems, **cooperation is the most successful strategy for survival** — not aggressive competition. "Survival of the fittest" in practice means "survival of the most cooperative." Aggression is the naive, short-term strategy that destroys the resource base.

---

## V. From Anthropocentric to Ecocentric Security

The security debate is trapped in anthropocentrism — it concerns only human survival. But we must think **ecocentrically.** A modern war is not just a catastrophe for humans; it is a death sentence for nature and every species we share the planet with. Radioactive fallout respects no borders.

Arne Naess's **ecosophy** teaches that nature's integrity is inseparable from our own security. Pacifism is therefore the ultimate environmental measure: by refusing to participate in the violence spiral, we protect not just human lives, but **the very foundation of life on Earth.**

---

## VI. The Declaration

> Maybe you're a pacifist without knowing it.

Even if you would defend your family in a critical moment, it doesn't mean you can't choose pacifism as the national strategy that gives the **highest probability** that such a moment never arises.

Pacifism is not about passivity. It is about actively creating conditions where nonviolent solutions become possible and likely. The best response to great-power rivalry is to take an active stand against **the very institution of war itself.**

Choosing pacifism is not giving up. It is choosing the strategy that maximizes the chance of a good outcome for all.

**The most logical path to peace is, ironically, to stop preparing for war.**
