## Prologue

*A response to those who believe that without military power, one becomes a puppet for others' will.*

We write from the perspectives of **human pain, ecology, and technological development** — a doctor treating soldiers wounded in Ukraine, Gaza, and Syria; an environmental advisor watching Norwegian nature suffer daily; and a nanotechnologist working toward a better ocean. It is from these vantage points that we regard the senselessness of war.

---

## On Puppets and Power

The claim that without military might one becomes a "puppet" rests on a confusion of terms. Hannah Arendt drew a sharp distinction between **Power** (cooperation, consensus) and **Violence** (coercion). An occupier can deploy violence but possesses **no power** if the population refuses to cooperate.

Pacifism is not submission. It is an **active deprivation of the occupier's power base.**

The real question: is one not *already* a puppet when sending soldiers to absurd alliance operations driven by superpower interests rather than world peace?

---

## The Biology Correction

The assumption that aggression is "realism" while cooperation is "naiveté" is a biological misunderstanding. Martin Nowak at Harvard has shown mathematically that in complex systems, **cooperation is the most successful survival strategy** — not aggressive competition.

*"Survival of the fittest"* in practice means **"survival of the most cooperative."** Aggression is the naive, short-term strategy that destroys the resource base it depends on.

---

## On Mike Tyson and Epistemology

A military respondent once quoted Mike Tyson — *"Everyone has a plan until they get punched in the face"* — to elevate tactical experience over theory.

This is an **epistemological fallacy.** Tactical experience with physical pain provides zero strategic insight into state security. As Kahneman demonstrates in *Thinking, Fast and Slow*, intuitive "gut feeling" (System 1) systematically loses to theoretical models (System 2) in complex risk analysis.

A broken nose is certainly educational — at the pain clinic we see daily how much can be learned from suffering. But we prefer **risk analyses over diagnoses** as guidelines for national defense policy.

---

## The Opportunity Cost

As Dwight D. Eisenhower — a man who knew war better than most — said:

> *"Every gun that is made, every warship launched, every rocket fired signifies, in the final sense, a theft from those who hunger and are not fed, those who are cold and are not clothed."*

From an ecological and medical standpoint, the arms race is an enormous waste of resources desperately needed to solve the climate crisis and global health challenges. Every dollar spent on a missile is a dollar **not** spent on antibiotics, clean water, or reforestation.

## Three Endings

We cannot run prospective randomized trials on world peace. So let us speculate on three possible endings:

### The Cruel Ending
The hippie-pacifists sang and sang around the bonfire. They had a wonderful time, but society ground to a halt. When the cruel occupiers arrived, the few starving hippies remaining were killed — too exhausted to even whisper *"peace and love."*

### The Kind Ending
All soldiers in the world stopped fighting and traded rifles and nuclear bombs for **ukuleles and kazoos.** Some even learned banjo. Many retrained as healthcare workers in an overburdened system. They worked in nursing homes, changed diapers, and helped elderly people. They found happiness in meaningful work. Everything became so much better that there was never an invasion or need for a military.

### The Probable Ending
Things took time. There was never a pacifist doctrine adopted nationally, but the debate around pacifism became slightly more visible in media. Unfortunately, more money went to the military and increased armament, but an invasion never came. There were fewer violent conflicts in the world overall — though those that arose were often linked to **climate change caused by human activity and increased militarization.**
