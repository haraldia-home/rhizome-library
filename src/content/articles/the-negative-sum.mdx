## Definitions

Let **S** represent the total security state of all nations. In classical game theory:

- **Zero-sum:** One's gain is another's loss. ΔS₁ = −ΔS₂
- **Positive-sum:** Cooperation yields surplus. ΔS_total > 0
- **Negative-sum:** All parties lose. ΔS_total < 0

The atomic age has introduced a permanent negative-sum backdrop to all military competition. In a modern great-power war, destruction would be so total that the sum becomes negative for **every participant.** There are no winners. Only ash.

## The Security Dilemma (Jervis, 1978)

In an anarchic international system, it is impossible for a neighboring state to distinguish between **defensive** and **offensive** weapons. When Nation A acquires a "bigger stick" to feel safe, Nation B interprets this as a threat and acquires an even bigger stick.

> The action designed to increase security **paradoxically decreases it** by raising the overall tension level.

The "big stick" strategy creates the very enemy it claims to protect against. This is not a political opinion. It is a **mathematical feedback loop** — a divergent series with no equilibrium point short of mutual destruction.

## Axelrod's Tournament (1984)

Robert Axelrod's iterated Prisoner's Dilemma tournaments demonstrated empirically that among all submitted strategies — hawkish, dovish, random, cunning — the winning strategy was the simplest:

> **Tit-for-Tat:** Cooperate first. Then mirror your opponent's last move.

The strategy that consistently maximized total payoff across all iterations was one that **began with trust** and only retaliated when provoked. Aggression-first strategies burned themselves out. Cooperation-first strategies accumulated compound returns.

The extension to geopolitics is immediate: **the only strategy that consistently reduces the probability of total loss is a coordinated movement toward disarmament.**

## Asymmetric Warfare (Arreguín-Toft, 2001)

Political scientist Ivan Arreguín-Toft analyzed asymmetric conflicts and found that when small states fight on a superpower's terms — conventional warfare — they **lose nearly every time.** The weak parties that win do so precisely by rejecting the strong party's logic, employing strategies that deny the enemy control.

Building a conventional defense against a nuclear superpower is **strategically irrational.** It invites a contest you are mathematically doomed to lose.

## The Promethean Gap (Anders, 1956)

Philosopher Günther Anders described what he called the **Promethean Gap**: our technological capacity to produce destruction has outrun our capacity to imagine the consequences.

In an era of long-range missiles and autonomous algorithms, concepts like "courage" and "cowardice" become **obsolete.** You cannot show heroism against a swarm of drones. Preparing for a "heroic" defensive battle is a romantic anachronism that ignores the reality of industrial mass death.

## Violence as Epidemic (Slutkin / WHO)

The World Health Organization and Gary Slutkin's **Cure Violence** model define violence as a contagious disease. Violence begets violence through exposure. Meeting violence with more violence — military escalation — is equivalent to attempting to extinguish an epidemic **by spreading the virus.**

The only cure is to break the chain of transmission through prevention and norm change, not through threats.

## Theorem

Given:
1. Nuclear arsenals ensure all military conflicts trend toward negative-sum outcomes
2. The security dilemma guarantees that armament escalation is a divergent, self-reinforcing series
3. Cooperation-first strategies dominate all iterated game-theoretic tournaments
4. Asymmetric resistance outperforms conventional defense for small states
5. Violence propagates epidemically; escalation amplifies the disease

**Therefore:** The rational strategy for a small nation is not to acquire a bigger stick, but to **exit the game entirely** — transforming the negative-sum contest into a positive-sum collaboration through coordinated disarmament and nonviolent resistance infrastructure.
