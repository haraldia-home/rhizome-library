## Prelude: The Chromatic Clock

In 1960, John Coltrane drew a diagram. It was not staff notation. It was a **circle** — all twelve chromatic pitches arranged like hours on a clock face, connected by geometric lines that traced the symmetric divisions of the octave. Musicians who saw it called it the "Coltrane Circle." Mathematicians recognized it as a study in **modular arithmetic**. Ethnomusicologists, had they been invited, would have recognized something older: the colotomic architecture of Balinese gamelan.

This diagram was not a jazz innovation. It was a **rediscovery** — the moment Western harmonic thinking, after centuries of linear teleology (tension → resolution → cadence), bent back upon itself and became circular. Coltrane had arrived, by pure harmonic logic, at the same temporal geometry that Southeast Asian and West African musicians had been inhabiting for millennia.

## I. Tenzer's Topology: A Continuum of Periodicity

Michael Tenzer's cross-cultural topology of musical time provides the necessary framework. Tenzer rejects the crude binary of "Western = linear" versus "Non-Western = cyclic," proposing instead a **nuanced continuum** based on the nature of periodicity itself:

- **Isochronous periodicity** — an evenly spaced pulse grid (the "beat" of Western pop, the strict tempo of a military march)
- **Non-isochronous periodicity** — a consistent cycle with asymmetric internal pulses (the "limping" rhythms of the Balkans, the additive timelines of West Africa)
- **Discursive non-isochrony** — free, breathing time without a fixed grid (the rubato introduction of a Balinese *kebyar*)

What makes Coltrane extraordinary is that **his career traverses the entire continuum.** *Giant Steps* (1959) is hyper-isochronous — the harmonic rhythm moves with mechanical precision through major-third cycles. *A Love Supreme* (1965) introduces sectional periodicity — open, non-metric invocation ("Acknowledgment") giving way to strict cyclic structure ("Resolution"). His final recordings dissolve into the discursive freedom of late free jazz.

He did not merely change styles. He **walked the full topology** of human temporal experience.

## II. The Coltrane Changes as Macro-Periodic Cycles

The "Coltrane Changes" — the harmonic substitution system that powers *Giant Steps* — are conventionally analyzed as chord progressions. But this misses their deeper architecture.

The system divides the octave into **three equal parts** (major thirds: C → E → A♭ → C), creating a **symmetric chromatic cycle**. Each tonal center is reached by a ii-V approach, producing a harmonic rhythm that is relentless, uniform, and — crucially — **circular**. There is no true "home key." The progression is a wheel.

> In Tenzer's framework, this is **strict isoperiodicity** — a single rhythmic and harmonic cycle that repeats without sectional contrast.

This structure is formally identical to the **colotomic principle** of Balinese gamelan, where a melodic cycle (*gongan*) repeats continuously, punctuated at fixed intervals by gong strokes that mark structural time. The gong does not "resolve" the melody any more than Coltrane's return to the starting key "resolves" the chord cycle. Both are **regenerative** — the end is the beginning.

## III. The Ewe Bell Pattern and the Standard Timeline

The connection deepens when we examine West African polyrhythm. The Ewe *gankogui* (double iron bell) plays a 12-pulse asymmetric timeline:

> **2 + 2 + 3 + 2 + 3**

This timeline is the **phrasing referent** for the entire ensemble. All other instruments orient their patterns in relation to the bell, just as Coltrane's improvisations orient in relation to the underlying harmonic cycle.

The critical parallel: both systems create **multiple simultaneous periodicities** nested within a single master cycle. In Ewe music, the master drum plays patterns that phase against the bell timeline, creating polyrhythmic tension. In Coltrane's *Giant Steps*, the improvised melody phases against the harmonic cycle, creating melodic tension that resolves only when the cycle regenerates.

Neither system is "about" arrival at a destination. Both are about **the quality of movement within the cycle itself.**

## IV. *A Love Supreme*: The Sacred Cycle

If *Giant Steps* is Coltrane's gamelan — strict, mechanical, isoperiodic — then *A Love Supreme* is his **Agbekor**. The four-movement suite moves through Tenzer's entire topology:

**"Acknowledgment"** opens with free, non-metric bass and cymbal — pure discursive non-isochrony. Coltrane's famous four-note motif ("a love su-preme") emerges gradually, stated in all twelve keys — **the chromatic circle made vocal**, a sacred circumnavigation of the tonal universe.

**"Resolution"** snaps into strict isoperiodic time — a walking bass line and driving rhythm section that provide the stable temporal container for Coltrane's most lyrical playing.

**"Pursuance"** introduces furious, almost non-metric drumming before locking into an aggressive cycle — **the war dance**, the equivalent of Agbekor's martial energy.

**"Psalm"** dissolves entirely into free time — Coltrane playing his tenor as speech, following the syllabic rhythm of a poem he had written. The cycle is broken. Linear time returns — but transformed by the journey through circularity.

> The suite is a **cosmological act**: the organization of all possible temporal experiences into a single, unified devotional structure.

## V. Balkan Asymmetries and the Limping Sublime

The topology extends beyond Africa and Asia. The **aksak** rhythms of the Balkans — the "limping" meters of Bulgarian and Turkish music — offer another dimension of non-isochronous periodicity that illuminates Coltrane's late work.

Bulgarian *kopanitsa* (11/16, grouped as 2+2+3+2+2) and *rachenitsa* (7/8, grouped as 2+2+3) demonstrate that a cycle can be consistent without being symmetric. The "limp" is not a defect — it is a **groove**, a form of forward momentum that feels fundamentally different from the stability of isochrony.

Coltrane's later recordings, particularly with Rashied Ali's polyrhythmic drumming, increasingly abandon isochronous time in favor of something analogous to aksak — **pulsed but asymmetric**, cycling but off-balance. The "chaos" that horrified jazz purists was, from a cross-cultural perspective, simply a migration toward the temporal logic that Balkan village musicians had always inhabited.

## VI. The Universal Grammar

The convergence is too precise to be coincidental. Coltrane's chromatic circle, the Balinese *gongan*, the Ewe bell timeline, the Balkan aksak — all are manifestations of a single cognitive imperative: **the organization of time into cycles.**

Karl Friston's Free Energy Principle suggests that the brain is a prediction machine, constantly minimizing surprise. Cyclic structures are the most neurologically efficient temporal organization — they allow the brain to predict, entrain, and anticipate. The "groove" we feel in music is literally **the pleasure of successful temporal prediction.**

This is why a 12/8 bell pattern played by an Ewe master drummer and a chromatic major-third cycle played by a jazz saxophonist in a New York studio can produce the same deep, physical response in a listener from any culture. They are activating the same **biological substrate** — the temporal prediction circuits that evolved with the Hmmmmm system of our Paleolithic ancestors.

Coltrane did not invent a new harmony. He rediscovered the geometry of time that the forest, the gamelan, and the bell had always known.

> The mandala was never his. It was always ours.
