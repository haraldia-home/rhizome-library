The definition of madness is doing the same thing repeatedly while expecting a different result. In modern security policy, this madness now carries a price tag of hundreds of billions.

**We meet a new era with old reflexes.** While the world changes radically, we continue to arm ourselves as if the calendar showed 1985.

## The Wrong War

Professor Tormod Heier at the Norwegian Military Academy has sounded the alarm: the threat does not come as tanks across the northern plateau. It comes through **cheap and effective hybrid pinpricks** — cable cuts, cyberattacks, power outages, and influence campaigns that sow mistrust and divide populations.

Despite this, astronomical sums flow into new frigates, submarines, and tanks — investments aimed at what Heier himself calls a *"worst possible, but least likely"* scenario.

It is difficult to describe this as anything other than a **strategic windmill battle** — a modern parallel to Don Quixote's heroic but hopeless knightly quest. He attacked imaginary giants with armor and lance. We attack hybrid threats with frigates.

> *"La verdad adelgaza y no quiebra, y siempre anda sobre la mentira como el aceite sobre el agua."*
> Truth can be stretched, but not broken — and it always floats above lies, like oil on water.

---

## The Ecosophic Blind Spot

Security cannot be reduced to pure military capacity. It must be understood as **a relationship between people, nature, and society.** A total defense without ecology, social sustainability, and animal welfare is only half a defense. It's like standing in full armor — but without shoes.

This lack of holistic thinking is evident everywhere. Funds intended for climate are quietly redirected: offshore wind becomes "energy security in war." Seabed mineral extraction is no longer about batteries — it's about independence from China. **When sustainability is sacrificed on the altar of security, we lose sight of science's true mission: ensuring humanity's long-term survival.**

We possess detailed military maps of the seabed for submarine warfare, but lack basic knowledge of the ecosystems we sacrifice. We know how to destroy an enemy, but precious little about how to preserve the foundation of life. *What will we live on when the war is over, if nature is dead?*

## The Brain Drain

Unni Eikeseth demonstrates in *The Laboratory of War* how researchers are drawn into weapons development. Every hour a top researcher spends optimizing a missile is an hour **not** spent solving the climate crisis or developing new antibiotics. We create an internal brain drain where the sharpest minds are locked into military projects while existential threats remain understaffed.

And we cement the error physically. Billions invested in infrastructure — laboratories and shipyards designed specifically for yesterday's war — create **path dependence**. Once we've built facilities for heavy weapons, the path to repurposing them for environmental solutions is blocked. We are building ourselves into a future where we are heavily armed but helpless against nature's collapse.

---

## The Real Defense Line

When we discuss total defense, we must talk about the welfare state — not abstractly, but specifically. A society is resilient when its people are secure. A country where nursing homes are crumbling, where the disabled live in constant economic uncertainty, where youth fall out of school — that country is **easy to destabilize.**

A society that doesn't care for its vulnerable makes itself vulnerable. Total defense doesn't begin at the military base. **It begins in kindergarten, in the health sector, at the welfare office, in the nursing home** — in all the institutions that hold us together before a crisis arrives.

We suffer from technological overconfidence that "smarter bombs" provide security. But missiles cannot repair trust crises or stop disinformation. **We're arming ourselves with steel against problems that require solidarity.** It's like trying to sew together an open wound with a hammer.

---

## The Verdict

Let us dare to see the truth: **a robust society is not built primarily by weapon systems, but by knowledge, trust, and welfare.**

Norway is arming for the wrong war.
