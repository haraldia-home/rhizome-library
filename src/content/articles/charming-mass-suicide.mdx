It is refreshing when people dare to say it outright: **We need more doomsday machines in Norwegian fjords.**

Nuclear weapons are "peace-keeping," they tell us — a kind of extreme variant of neighbor-conflict logic. When humanistic arguments bounce off, when game theory is dismissed, perhaps we must return to the fundamental physics: the kind of knowledge that those who eagerly want "the button" have rarely lingered over long enough.

## Oppenheimer's Precedent

When Robert Oppenheimer and his team at Los Alamos were still in the theoretical phase of the Manhattan Project, they were not sure whether they were going to **burn up the entire planet**. They seriously discussed the possibility that a fission explosion could trigger a self-sustaining chain reaction in the nitrogen of the atmosphere. The fear was literal: they might set fire to the air we breathe, transforming the Earth into a burning star in a fraction of a second.

They calculated that the chance was "very small" — and then **detonated the bomb anyway.**

This is the gambler mentality we now wish to invite into defense policy: a philosophy where *"probably it will be okay"* is good enough when the stakes are all life.

## The Upgrade: From Atmospheric Fire to Reality Deletion

But physics has come a long way since 1945. Oppenheimer was merely a pessimist with outdated mathematics. Today we know exactly how a proper nuclear exchange can trigger what theorists call **"The Ping of Death"** — a six-step, peer-reviewable program for uninstalling reality itself.

We're no longer talking about something as *banal* as setting fire to the air. We're talking about vacuum decay, Kugelblitz collapse, Gregory-Moss catalysis, and the synchronous formatting of every black hole in the multiverse. The details are in the theorem — the mathematics hold under scrutiny.

## The Paasilinna Bus

This brings to mind Finnish author **Arvo Paasilinna** and his masterpiece *Collective Suicide*. A motley group of Finns embark on a bus tour through Europe to end it all together, with a kind of absurd, nationalist pride in their own ending. The difference is that Paasilinna's characters at least have a tragicomic dignity — a hint of human fellowship in their madness.

Our current geopolitical strategy appears as a global version of **Paasilinna's suicide bus**, except we've replaced the bus with intercontinental missiles controlled by a computer, and the passengers haven't been asked if they want to come along. We all sit in the seats while drivers squabble about who has the right to drive over the cliff first, all while assuring us that *speed is a guarantee of our security*.

## The Punch Line

There is a dark irony that these weapons are marketed as guarantees of security. In reality they are a guarantee that if something goes wrong, it goes wrong in a way that makes the word "catastrophe" **insufficient**. We're talking about total indifference to nature, to animals, and to the next ten thousand generations of civilians who must pay for our inability to think outside the nuclear box.

Call it what it is: **A collective suicide pact camouflaged as geopolitical strategy.**

So yes, by all means — say yes to nuclear weapons! And hope that the laws of physics are more forgiving than our own stupidity. Let us gamble that the atmosphere holds, that the quantum vacuum is stable enough to tolerate our aggression, and that we don't accidentally rip the universe apart because we couldn't come up with anything better than *"an eye for an eye"* in megaton scale.

But don't call it defense.

Call it **"Hurmaava joukkoitsemurha"** — *Charming Mass Suicide* in Finnish.
